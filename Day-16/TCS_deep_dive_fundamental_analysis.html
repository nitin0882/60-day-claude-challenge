Business overview

TCS is India's largest IT services company and the Tata Group's crown jewel, founded in 1968. It serves 600,000+ employees across 150 locations in 46 countries, offering IT outsourcing, consulting, cloud, AI/GenAI, BFSI platforms, and digital transformation services. Major verticals include Banking/Financial Services, Retail, Life Sciences, Communications, and Manufacturing. Tata Sons owns ~71.8% and relies on TCS for ~95% of its group dividend income, making TCS the financial backbone of the entire Tata conglomerate.

Strengths
Fortress balance sheet. Near-zero debt (D/E 0.09), ₹45,922 Cr net cash, and massive FCF generation make TCS essentially un-ratable as a financial risk. The company doesn't need banks.
Best-in-class capital efficiency. ROE of 51.8% and ROCE of 63% — achieved without leverage — signal genuine competitive advantages in operations and client retention.
Irreplaceable scale and relationships. 584,000+ employees, $40.7B deal TCV in FY26, and 50+ year client relationships create switching costs that are virtually impossible to replicate.
AI monetisation in progress. Annualised AI revenue of $2.3B is real, growing, and ahead of most peers in absolute scale. TCS's wide deployment base gives it data and use-case advantages.
Generous shareholder returns. ₹457/share cumulative dividends over 5 years, two buybacks, and 5% dividend yield at current prices — rare in large-cap growth companies.
Watch-points
Revenue growth slump. FY26 constant-currency revenue grew ~2.4% — barely ahead of inflation. If global IT spending stagnates further, TCS has limited levers to accelerate growth given its size.
US macro and tariff risk. ~53% of TCS revenue comes from North America. Any slowdown in US discretionary IT spend, or tariff-related client cost cuts, directly compresses TCS's topline.
AI disruption to headcount model. If AI tools reduce the number of billable engineers needed per project, TCS's labour-arbitrage model faces structural pressure over a 3-5 year horizon.
Stock de-rating is sharp. Down 38% in 1 year — despite improving margins. While valuation looks cheap vs. history, further growth disappointments could keep P/E compressed.

Key metric to track

Constant currency revenue growth (quarterly) is the single most important number. A sustained return above 5–6% CC growth would signal a re-rating catalyst. Also watch AI revenue trajectory and North America segment recovery.
